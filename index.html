LAMaRC

Thermodynamic Engineering of Metastable Materials Systems

Fundamental materials science integrating solid-state processing, atomic-scale characterization, and quantitative analysis of defect-mediated phase stability. LAMaRC investigates how thermodynamic driving forces, structural defects, and interfacial chemistry govern the stability and evolution of metastable materials systems. By combining non-equilibrium solid-state processing with nanoscale characterization and functional property measurement, the laboratory establishes predictive relationships between processing pathways, microstructural hierarchy, and performance in extreme environments.

Scientific Focus

LAMaRC advances a fundamental understanding of metastability in structural and functional materials. Processing is treated as a thermodynamic perturbation capable of altering free-energy landscapes, redistributing chemical species, and stabilizing non-equilibrium microstructures.

The research program integrates solid-state processing and severe plastic deformation, diffusion-mediated phase transformations, defect thermodynamics and interfacial chemistry, atomic-scale and nanoscale characterization, and quantitative evaluation of mechanical, thermal, magnetic, and electrochemical response.

The objective is to design materials systems whose microstructural hierarchy — from atomic defects to mesoscale architecture — is engineered for controlled evolution rather than unintended degradation.

Laboratory

LAMaRC (Lab for Agile Manufacturing and Rapid Characterization) integrates solid-state synthesis and nanoscale characterization to enable controlled microstructural reconfiguration in metallic and multifunctional materials.

Core capabilities include solid-state shear processing and consolidation, severe plastic deformation and non-equilibrium synthesis, diffusion-controlled phase stability engineering, atomic-scale microscopy and atom probe tomography, and nano-mechanical and functional property characterization.

Processing pathways are developed to introduce controlled defect populations, manipulate interfacial energetics, and induce localized chemical redistribution without bulk melting. These engineered perturbations provide access to metastable phases and hierarchical microstructures not attainable through equilibrium processing routes.

Research Themes

Metastability & Defect Thermodynamics

Quantifying how dislocations, chemical short-range order, interfacial segregation, and non-equilibrium defect populations influence phase transformations and ordering transitions in complex materials systems.

Solid-State Processing as Thermodynamic Control

Employing friction-based processing and severe plastic deformation as mechanisms for reshaping free-energy landscapes and stabilizing engineered metastable states.

Atomic-Scale Characterization of Microstructural Evolution

Applying advanced electron microscopy and atom probe tomography to resolve nanoscale chemical gradients, defect structures, and interfacial chemistry under coupled stimuli.

Interface-Controlled Functional Response

Establishing structure–property relationships governing thermal transport, magnetic behavior, mechanical deformation, and electrochemical stability.

Stability Under Extreme Conditions

Mapping microstructural evolution under thermal, mechanical, and irradiation extremes to enable materials systems capable of sustained and predictable performance.
